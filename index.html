Outline

"Data Science" is more than a buzzword, or a re-dressing of existing ideas from statistics and computer science; it's an extremely useful way of looking at the research process from the perspective of the data pipeline. In this class we will be examining the Data Science process from the perspective of social science research.

Course Objectives:

  1. Outline the Data Science process in terms of tasks to accomplish and the tools we will use to make them work.
  2. Effectively construct analysis-ready data sets that are portable and appropriately annotated.
  3. Analyze data through statistical and graphical means.
  4. Effectively use R, a widely-used statistical package, to perform analyses, integrated with other software packages.
We will combine the lecture- and lab-styles in this class. Students are expected to bring their laptop computers to every class and spend some of this class time working on the homework assignments in groups.

Final Project Deliverables Guide

The bulk of the project was covered in Homeworks 8, 9 and 10 (in Lectures 10-12). A reminder that the general expectation of the project is replication: How would you show someone else how to replicate the work you’ve done? Recall the specific research question: Do the emotional and subjective qualifications of the tweets of “die-hard” fans of a college team correlate to the team’s performance in college football games – either before or after the game is played?

This performance includes: winning the game, winning by 7 or more points, losing by 7 or more points, and exceeding or beating the spread.

Specific project deliverables:

  1. Long Form – in the style of the homeworks: a document detailing the full creation of the project.

    • How you obtained and saved the data of interest for your project. This includes code blocks that do not need to be run directly when you knit (that is, you should use the eval=FALSE option), especially if you are saving the data locally. For the data you copied directly from online, you should make it clear what the original source was for anyone else who might be interested in repeating the process.

    • How you processed the data into what you needed to perform your analyses. Regular expression matching, table joins, explicit inclusion from other sources. This should include a description of all the data you’ve assembled and how you made exceptions for data that did not make sense. What would someone doing this project again expect to see?

    • The actual analyses you’ve conducted. This can include the logistic model you were asked for but can also include simple statistical comparisons, graphical summaries and anything else you hope will clarify the analysis and its results.

    • The conclusions you’ve drawn from the data you’ve put together: positive, negative and null.

  1. Executive Summary document. This should be no longer than 3 printed pages long and should contain no visible code, only what you need to describe the work you’ve done to anyone interested in the research. Graphics would be helpful here to illustrate the points you’ve made. This will be what you present in class on December 7.

  2. The supplemental data files you have created to answer these questions.
